\section{Motivation and Design Objectives}
\label{sec:heuristics_motivation}
\hspace{\parindent}
The motivation for developing prioritization heuristics arises from the fact that the same planning instance can produce substantially different results depending only on the order in which robots are planned. In constrained environments, assigning priority to one robot may reduce its own planning difficulty while increasing the constraints imposed on the remaining robots.

Since the best ordering depends on robot distribution, goals, roadmap structure, and path interactions, no single prioritization rule is expected to perform well in all scenarios. For this reason, the objective of this work is not to identify one dominant heuristic, but to design a diverse set of complementary ordering strategies. These heuristics should produce priority orderings that are informative enough to improve planning feasibility or solution quality, while remaining computationally lightweight when compared with the execution time of TEA*. Given that prioritization is performed before planner execution, excessive heuristic computation time may reduce or even eliminate the practical gains obtained from improved orderings.
\section{Heuristic Design Methodology}
\label{sec:heuristic_design_methodology}
\hspace{\parindent}
The heuristics developed in this work follow a common score-based formulation. Given a set of robots

\begin{equation}
    R = \{r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_n\},
\end{equation}
each heuristic assigns a priority score, $score_i$, to every robot, $r_i$, according to the information available in the current planning instance. For a given heuristic, the priority score assigned to robot \(r_i\) is denoted as:

\begin{equation}
    score_i = h(d_i),
\end{equation}
where \(d_i\) denotes the input data used by the heuristic to evaluate robot \(r_i\). 

Depending on the heuristic being applied, \(d_i\) may include information such as the robot's current position, goal vertex, estimated path, predicted conflicts, roadmap-related properties, or data from previous coordination cycles.

After computing the scores, the priority ordering is obtained using a common sorting procedure. The sorting direction is defined by the parameter \(mode\). In the \(\textit{First}\) mode, robots with higher scores are planned earlier, while in the \(\textit{Last}\) mode, robots with lower scores are planned earlier. This operation is denoted as

\begin{equation}
    \pi = \textsc{SortByScore}(R, score, mode),
\end{equation}
where \(\pi\) represents the resulting priority permutation of the robots.

The developed heuristics are organized into five main groups according to the type of information they exploit:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Roadmap distance heuristics}, based on distances or estimated path lengths in the roadmap graph;

    \item \textbf{Topological heuristics}, based on structural properties of the graph and its constrained regions;

    \item \textbf{Surroundings-based heuristics}, based on the spatial distribution of robots around their current positions or goals;

    \item \textbf{Conflict-based heuristics}, based on estimated interactions between tentative robot paths;

    \item \textbf{Replanning heuristics}, based on information from previous plans or coordination cycles.
\end{itemize}

Together, these groups cover complementary dimensions of the coordination problem, allowing the proposed heuristics to address robot prioritization from different perspectives. Each group is examined in the following sections, where the corresponding heuristics are described in terms of their motivation, scoring procedure, and computational implications.

\section{Roadmap Distance Heuristics}
\label{sec:roadmap_distance_heuristics}
\hspace{\parindent}
Roadmap distance heuristics define robot priority orderings using distance measures between each
robot and its goal. Following the Roadmap Distance nomenclature reported by Heselden and
Das~\cite{machines2023survey}, the two heuristics developed in this group correspond to
\emph{Roadmap Distance 1}, where the farthest robot is given priority, and
\emph{Roadmap Distance 2}, where the robot with the longest path is planned first.

These are the simplest heuristics considered in this work, since they rely only on travel distance
as an approximate indicator of planning difficulty. Although computationally lightweight, they do
not explicitly account for path conflicts, local robot density, or topological constraints. The two
implemented criteria are the direct distance between each robot and its goal and the length of an
estimated shortest path in the roadmap graph, giving rise to the \textit{Farthest Robot} and
\textit{Longest Path} heuristics, respectively.


\subsection{Farthest Robot}
\label{subsec:furthest_robot}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{Farthest Robot} heuristic assigns priorities according to the direct distance between each robot and its goal. 

For each robot $r_i$, let $s_i = (x_i, y_i)$ denote its current position and let $g_i = (x_{g_i}, y_{g_i})$ denote its goal position. The score assigned to robot $r_i$ is computed as the Euclidean distance between these two points:

\begin{equation}
    score_i = dist(s_i, g_i) = \|g_i - s_i\|_2
\end{equation}

Figure~\ref{fig:furthest_robot_heuristic} illustrates this computation for a single robot and goal pair. In the example, the score assigned to robot $r_1$ corresponds to the direct distance between its current position, $s_1$, and its goal position, $g_1$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{furthest.png}
    \caption{Score computation for the Farthest Robot heuristic.}
    \label{fig:furthest_robot_heuristic}
\end{figure}



\subsubsection{Ordering variants}
\label{subsubsec:furthest_robot_ordering_variants}
\hspace{\parindent}
Two ordering variants were considered. In the \textit{Farthest Robot First} variant, robots with higher distance scores are planned earlier, following the intuition that longer movements should be planned before the search space becomes more constrained.

In the \textit{Farthest Robot Last} variant, the ordering is reversed, and robots with lower distance scores are planned earlier. This strategy may be useful in scenarios where the objective is to maximize the number of robots that successfully reach their goals, especially when a complete solution for all robots is difficult or infeasible. By prioritizing robots closer to their goals, the planner may allow a larger subset of robots to complete their paths before the remaining planning problem becomes too constrained.

\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:furthest_robot_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
The procedure is summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:furthest_robot}. For each robot, the heuristic computes the Euclidean distance between its current roadmap position and its goal vertex, and then applies the sorting procedure to obtain the final priority ordering.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Farthest Robot Heuristic}
\label{alg:furthest_robot}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Set of robots \(R\), current closest-vertex positions \(s_i\) on the roadmap, current goal vertices \(g_i\) on the roadmap, ordering mode \(mode\)
\Ensure Priority ordering \(\pi\)

\For{each robot \(r_i \in R\)}
    \State \(score_i \gets dist(s_i, g_i)\)
\EndFor

\State \(\pi \gets \textsc{SortByScore}(R, score, mode)\)

\State \Return \(\pi\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

In the following complexity analyses, \(n = |R|\) denotes the number of robots. Each score is computed in constant time, resulting in \(O(n)\) time for all robots. The final score-based sorting step requires \(O(n \log n)\) time. Thus, the total computational cost is

\begin{equation}
    O(n) + O(n \log n)
\end{equation}
which is dominated by the sorting step.

\subsection{Longest Path}
\label{subsec:longest_path}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{Longest Path} heuristic assigns priorities according to the cost of an estimated shortest path on the roadmap graph. Unlike the \textit{Farthest Robot} heuristic, which uses only the Euclidean distance between the current and goal vertices, this heuristic accounts for the actual connectivity of the roadmap. This is particularly relevant in scenarios where the Euclidean distance does not reflect the real distance that the robot must travel, for example when obstacles force the robot to follow a longer route through the graph.


For each robot \(r_i\), a single-robot shortest path is computed between its current vertex \(s_i\) and its goal vertex \(g_i\) using \(A^*\). The resulting path is represented as

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:astar_path}
    P_i^\ast = (v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_k),
\end{equation}
with \(v_1 = s_i\) and \(v_k = g_i\). The priority score of robot \(r_i\) is then defined as the total cost of the edges along this path:

\begin{equation}
    score_i
    =
    \sum_{\ell=1}^{k-1} w_{v_\ell, v_{\ell+1}},
\end{equation}
where \(w_{v_\ell, v_{\ell+1}}\) denotes the weight of the edge connecting consecutive vertices \(v_\ell\) and \(v_{\ell+1}\).

In the example shown in Figure~\ref{fig:longest_path}, the path computed for robot \(r_1\) traverses the sequence of vertices from its initial position to its goal. The corresponding score is obtained by summing the costs of the selected roadmap edges.

\begin{equation}
    score_1 = w_{1,2} + w_{2,5} + w_{5,6} + w_{6,9}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.35\linewidth]{longest_path.png}
    \caption{Illustration of the Longest Path heuristic.}
    \label{fig:longest_path}
\end{figure}



\subsubsection{Ordering variants}
\label{subsubsec:longest_path_ordering_variants}
\hspace{\parindent}
Following the same logic used for the \textit{Farthest Robot} heuristic, two ordering variants were considered. In the \textit{Longest Path First} variant, robots with higher path-cost scores are planned earlier, while in the \textit{Longest Path Last} variant, robots with lower path-cost scores are planned earlier. Both variants use the same priority score definition and differ only in the sorting direction.

\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:longest_path_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:longest_path} summarizes the \textit{Longest Path} heuristic. For each robot, the algorithm computes a single-agent shortest path on the roadmap, uses the corresponding path cost as the priority score, and then applies the generic score-based sorting procedure to obtain the final ordering.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Longest Path Heuristic}
\label{alg:longest_path}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Set of robots \(R\), current positions \(s_i\), goal positions \(g_i\), ordering mode \(mode\)
\Ensure Priority ordering \(\pi\)

\For{each robot \(r_i \in R\)}
    \State \(p_i \gets A^*(s_i, g_i)\) \Comment{single-robot shortest path on the roadmap}
    \State \(score_i \gets cost(p_i)\) \Comment{sum of edge costs along \(p_i\)}
\EndFor

\State \(\pi \gets \textsc{SortByScore}(R, score, mode)\)

\State \Return \(\pi\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The dominant operation in this heuristic is the computation of one \(A^*\) path per robot. As discussed in Section~\ref{subsubsec:astar_search_algorithm}, in the worst case, \(A^*\) may explore the same search space as Dijkstra's algorithm. Therefore, using the complexity given in Equation~\ref{eq:dijkstra_complexity}, computing the priority scores for all robots requires \(O\bigl(n \cdot (|V| + |E|)\log |V|\bigr)\) time. Since the final score-based sorting step requires \(O(n \log n)\) time, the total computational cost is

\begin{equation}
    O\bigl(n \cdot (|V| + |E|)\log|V|\bigr) + O(n \log n),
\end{equation}
which is typically dominated by the \(n\) shortest-path computations.


\section{Topological Heuristics}
\label{sec:topological_heuristics}
\hspace{\parindent}
The topological heuristics developed in this work are motivated by the environment heuristics
discussed in Section~\ref{sec:agent_prioritization}, which assign priority according to structural
properties of the environment. Previous approaches capture this idea either by reasoning about meaningful route alternatives, as in
\emph{Path Prospects}, or by considering the expected demand on highly shared
infrastructure~\cite{wu2020pathprospects,termors2011evaluating}. However, the former requires
additional graph expansion and effective obstacle
identification, while the latter depends on prior
estimates of infrastructure demand.

In this work, a lighter alternative is adopted by estimating movement flexibility from the roadmap
path computed for each robot. This preserves the intuition that robots crossing structurally
constrained regions may be more affected by previous reservations, while avoiding the need for prior
statistics about infrastructure demand.

To quantify this notion of movement flexibility, the Degree of Freedom (DoF) of roadmap vertices is
used. Each roadmap vertex \(v\) is associated with a set of directly connected neighboring
vertices, denoted by \(N(v)\). The DoF of \(v\) is defined as

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{DoF}(v) = |N(v)|, 
\end{equation}
where \(|N(v)|\) represents the number of neighbors of vertex \(v\).

For each robot \(r_i\), the same single-robot shortest path notation introduced in
Section~\ref{subsec:longest_path} is used. A path \(P_i^\ast\) is computed between the current
vertex \(s_i\) and the goal vertex \(g_i\) using \(A^*\), and the vertices belonging to this path
are then evaluated according to their DoF values.

Vertices with lower DoF values typically correspond to more constrained regions of the environment,
such as corridors or narrow passages, where robots have fewer movement options. Conversely, vertices
with higher DoF values tend to represent more open regions of the roadmap, where robots have greater
flexibility to avoid conflicts. Figure~\ref{fig:topological_dof} illustrates this concept on a
roadmap path computed with \(A^*\).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{dof.png}
    \caption{Illustration of DoF values along a roadmap path.}
    \label{fig:topological_dof}
\end{figure}

Based on this information, two topological criteria are considered. The first summarizes the overall
movement flexibility of a path through its average DoF, while the second explicitly counts the
number of low-DoF vertices traversed by the path. These criteria give rise to the \textit{Average
DoF Path} and \textit{Low DoF Sum} heuristics, respectively.

\subsection{Average DoF Path}
\label{subsec:average_dof_path}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{Average DoF Path} heuristic assigns priorities according to the average topological flexibility of the roadmap path estimated for each robot. For each robot \(r_i\), a single-robot shortest path \(P_i^\ast\) is first computed between its current vertex \(s_i\) and its goal vertex \(g_i\). The DoF values of the vertices traversed by this path are then averaged to obtain the priority score.

Let \(V(P_i^\ast)\) denote the set of distinct roadmap vertices traversed by path \(P_i^\ast\). The score of robot \(r_i\) is defined as

\begin{equation}
    score_i =
    \frac{1}{|V(P_i^\ast)|}
    \sum_{v \in V(P_i^\ast)} \mathrm{DoF}(v).
\end{equation}

Therefore, the score corresponds to the mean roadmap connectivity along the estimated path of the robot. Higher scores indicate paths through more connected roadmap regions, while lower scores indicate more constrained paths with fewer movement alternatives.

This heuristic provides a compact measure of the overall flexibility of a path. However, because it is based on an average value, short but highly constrained portions of the path may be smoothed by more open regions.

Considering the path illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:topological_dof}, the Average DoF Path score for robot $r_1$ is computed as
\[
    \mathrm{score}(r_1) =
    \frac{
    \mathrm{DoF}(7) +
    \mathrm{DoF}(8) +
    \mathrm{DoF}(9) +
    \mathrm{DoF}(10) +
    \mathrm{DoF}(11) +
    \mathrm{DoF}(12)
    }{6}
    =
    3,
\]
where each $\mathrm{DoF}(v)$ denotes the connectivity of vertex $v$. Therefore, the score corresponds to the mean connectivity of the vertices traversed by the path.

\subsubsection{Ordering variants}
\label{subsubsec:average_dof_path_ordering_variants}
\hspace{\parindent}
Two ordering variants were considered. In the \textit{High Average DoF Path First} variant, robots with higher average DoF scores are planned earlier, prioritizing paths that traverse more open regions of the roadmap.
In the \textit{Low Average DoF Path First} variant, the ordering is reversed, and robots with lower average DoF scores are planned earlier, prioritizing paths that traverse more constrained regions. Both variants use the same score and differ only in the sorting direction.
\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:average_dof_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:average_dof} summarizes the \textit{Average DoF Path} heuristic. For each robot, the algorithm first computes an \(A^*\) path between the current and goal vertices. It then identifies the distinct roadmap vertices traversed by that path and evaluates the DoF of each one by counting its distinct neighboring vertices. These values are accumulated and averaged, producing a priority score that represents the mean level of movement flexibility available along the estimated path. The final ordering is obtained by applying the selected sorting mode to these scores.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Average DoF Path Heuristic}
\label{alg:average_dof}
\footnotesize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Set of robots $R$, roadmap graph $G$, current positions $s_i$, goal positions $g_i$, ordering mode $mode$
\Ensure Priority ordering $\pi$

\For{each robot $r_i \in R$}
    \State $p_i \gets A^*(s_i, g_i)$  \Comment{single-robot shortest path on the roadmap}

    \State $N_i \gets$ distinct vertices along $p_i$
    \State $DoFsum_i \gets 0$

    \For{each vertex $v \in N_i$}
        \State $N_v \gets \emptyset$ \Comment{set of distinct neighbors of $v$}
        \For{each edge $e$ in $\textsc{AdjacencyList}(v)$}
            \State $u \gets \textsc{OppositeEndpoint}(e, v)$ \Comment{neighbor reached through edge $e$}
            \State insert $u$ into $N_v$
        \EndFor
        \State $\mathrm{DoF}(v) \gets |N_v|$ \Comment{number of distinct neighbors of $v$}
        \State $DoFsum_i \gets DoFsum_i + \mathrm{DoF}(v)$
    \EndFor

    \State $score_i \gets DoFsum_i / |N_i|$
\EndFor

\State $\pi \gets \textsc{SortByScore}(R, score, mode)$
\State \Return $\pi$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


The dominant operations in this heuristic are the single-robot path searches and the evaluation of the DoF values along the resulting paths. Using the \(A^*\) worst-case complexity given in Equation~\ref{eq:dijkstra_complexity}, the path computation step is applied once per robot.

After each path is obtained, the heuristic visits its vertices and computes the DoF of each one by checking its adjacency list. If \(L_i\) is the number of edges in the path of robot \(r_i\), and \(\Delta = \max_{v \in V} \mathrm{DoF}(v)\) is the maximum degree of any roadmap vertex, the DoF aggregation step costs at most \(O(L_i \cdot \Delta)\) for that robot. Considering all robots, this cost is upper-bounded by

\begin{equation}
    O(n \cdot L_{\max} \cdot \Delta),
\end{equation}
where \(L_{\max} = \max_i L_i\). The final score-based sorting step requires \(O(n \log n)\) time. Thus, the total computational cost is given by

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:average_dof_heuristic}
    O\bigl(n \cdot (|V| + |E|)\log|V|\bigr)
    + O\bigl(n \cdot L_{\max} \cdot \Delta\bigr)
    + O(n \log n).
\end{equation}



\subsection{Low DoF Sum}
\label{subsec:low_dof_sum}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{Low DoF Sum} heuristic assigns priorities according to the number of constrained vertices traversed by the roadmap path of each robot. Instead of averaging the DoF values along the path, as in the \textit{Average DoF Path} heuristic, this approach explicitly counts how many vertices are classified as topologically restricted.

A vertex is considered constrained when its DoF is lower than or equal to a predefined threshold \(\tau\). The priority score of robot \(r_i\) is defined as

\begin{equation}
    score_i =
    \sum_{v \in P_i^\ast} \delta(v),
\end{equation}
with

\begin{equation}
\delta(v)=
\begin{cases}
1, & \mathrm{DoF}(v) \leq \tau, \\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Therefore, the score corresponds to the number of low-DoF vertices traversed by the path of the robot.

Compared with the \textit{Average DoF Path} heuristic, the \textit{Low DoF Sum} heuristic provides a more explicit measure of path restriction. While \textit{Average DoF Path} summarizes the overall topological flexibility of a path through a mean value, \textit{Low DoF Sum} directly counts the number of vertices that satisfy a predefined restriction criterion. As a result, it can better emphasize paths that cross several clearly constrained regions, even when the average DoF of the complete path is not particularly low.

This distinction can lead to different priority scores in scenarios where the distribution of constrained vertices along the path is relevant. For example, two paths may have similar average DoF values, but one may traverse several very restricted vertices while the other only passes through moderately constrained areas. In such cases, the \textit{Low DoF Sum} heuristic tends to penalize the first path more strongly, whereas the average-based score may smooth out this difference.

However, this explicitness also introduces a limitation. The heuristic depends directly on the threshold $\tau$, which defines what is considered a low-DoF vertex. Therefore, its behavior is more sensitive to parameter selection and to the specific connectivity characteristics of the roadmap. In contrast, the \textit{Average DoF Path} heuristic is more general, since it does not require a hard classification of vertices as constrained or unconstrained. 

Considering the path illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:topological_dof}, the threshold is set to $\tau = 2$. Therefore, only vertices with $\mathrm{DoF}(v) \leq 2$ are classified as constrained. In this case, vertices $9$ and $10$ satisfy the restriction condition, while vertices $7$, $8$, $11$, and $12$ do not. The Low DoF Sum score for robot $r_1$ is therefore:
\[
    \mathrm{score}(r_1) =
    \delta(7) +
    \delta(8) +
    \delta(9) +
    \delta(10) +
    \delta(11) +
    \delta(12)
    = 0 + 0 + 1 + 1 + 0 + 0 = 2.
\]

\subsubsection{Ordering variants}
\label{subsubsec:low_dof_sum_ordering_variants}
\hspace{\parindent}
Two ordering variants were considered. In the \textit{Low DoF Sum First} variant, robots whose paths traverse more constrained vertices (a higher score) are planned earlier, prioritizing the most restricted paths. In the \textit{Low DoF Sum Last} variant, the ordering is reversed, and robots with fewer constrained vertices are planned earlier. Both variants use the same score and differ only in the sorting direction.

\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:low_dof_sum_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:low_dof_sum} follows the same structure as Algorithm~\ref{alg:average_dof}, differing only in the score computation.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Low DoF Sum Heuristic}
\label{alg:low_dof_sum}
\footnotesize
\setstretch{0.95}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require robots \(R\), roadmap graph \(G\), current positions \(s_i\), goal positions \(g_i\), ordering mode \(mode\), DoF threshold \(\tau\)
\Ensure Priority ordering \(\pi\)

\For{each robot \(r_i \in R\)}
    \State \(p_i \gets A^*(s_i, g_i)\) \Comment{single-agent shortest path on the roadmap}
    \State \(N_i \gets\) distinct vertices along \(p_i\)
    \State \(score_i \gets 0\)

    \For{each vertex $v \in N_i$}
        \State $N_v \gets \emptyset$ \Comment{set of distinct neighbors of $v$}
        \For{each edge $e$ in $\textsc{AdjacencyList}(v)$}
            \State $u \gets \textsc{OppositeEndpoint}(e, v)$ \Comment{neighbor reached through edge $e$}
            \State insert $u$ into $N_v$
        \EndFor

        \State $\mathrm{DoF}(v) \gets |N_v|$ \Comment{number of distinct neighbors of $v$}

        \If{$\mathrm{DoF}(v) \leq \tau$}
            \State $score_i \gets score_i + 1$ \Comment{count constrained vertex}
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
\EndFor

\State \(\pi \gets \textsc{SortByScore}(R, score, mode)\)

\State \Return \(\pi\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

This heuristic has the same computational complexity as the \textit{Average DoF Path} heuristic. The only difference lies in the aggregation step, since \textit{Low DoF Sum} counts the vertices that satisfy \(\mathrm{DoF}(v) \leq \tau\) instead of averaging the DoF values. Therefore, the total computational cost remains essentially the same as in Equation~\ref{eq:average_dof_heuristic}.

\section{Surroundings Heuristics}
\label{sec:surroundings_heuristics}
\hspace{\parindent}
The surroundings heuristics developed in this work are inspired by local-surroundings priority
criteria discussed in Section~\ref{sec:agent_prioritization}. In particular, they follow the same
general intuition as \emph{Naive Surroundings}, where priority is influenced by how crowded the
region around a robot is. However, instead of counting nearby obstacles, the heuristics proposed
here evaluate the spatial distribution of other robots around a selected reference point.

The selected reference point can correspond either to the current position of the robot or to its
goal position. When the current position is used, the heuristic estimates local congestion at the
beginning of the planning process. When the goal position is used, it estimates goal concentration,
capturing cases where several robots may compete for access to the same final area or to nearby
roadmap resources.

Thus, these heuristics adapt the surroundings principle to the spatial distribution of robots,
measuring local crowding through radius, roadmap, or cluster neighborhoods.

\subsection{Radius Neighbor Density}
\label{subsec:radius_neighbor_density}
\hspace{\parindent}
The Radius Neighbor Density heuristic assigns priorities according to the local density of robots around a selected reference point. For each robot, the heuristic counts how many other robots are located within a predefined distance threshold. This provides a simple proximity-based estimate of how crowded the surrounding region is.

Let $n_i$ denote the reference point associated with robot $r_i$, which can correspond either to its current position or to its goal position, depending on the variant being evaluated. Given a distance threshold $R_{\text{th}}$, the score of robot $r_i$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    score_i =
    \sum_{\substack{r_j \in R \\ j \neq i}}
    \rho(i,j),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\rho(i,j)=
\begin{cases}
1, & d(n_i,n_j) \leq R_{\text{th}}, \\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Here, $d(n_i,n_j)$ represents the Euclidean distance between the reference points associated with robots $r_i$ and $r_j$. Therefore, the score corresponds to the number of neighboring robots located within radius $R_{\text{th}}$ of robot $r_i$.

Robots with higher scores are located in denser local regions, since more robots are found within their neighborhood. When the current position is used as the reference point, this may indicate robots that may have more difficulty initiating their movements due to the presence of nearby agents. When the goal position is used instead, higher scores indicate goals located in more concentrated areas, where several robots may compete for access to nearby roadmap resources.

This heuristic is strongly affected by the choice of the radius threshold. If $R_{\text{th}}$ is too small, most robots may have few or no neighbors, making the heuristic unable to distinguish between different local configurations. Conversely, if $R_{\text{th}}$ is too large, many robots may be counted as neighbors, reducing the locality of the measure. Therefore, the value of $R_{\text{th}}$ directly controls the scale at which local density is evaluated.

\subsubsection{Ordering variants}
\label{subsubsec:radius_neighbor_density_ordering_variants}
\hspace{\parindent}
Two ordering variants were considered for the \textit{Radius Neighbor Density} heuristic. The first one, \textit{High Radius Density Goals First}, computes the density score using the goal positions of the robots and prioritizes robots whose goals are located in denser regions. This strategy is motivated by the behavior of TEA* in scenarios where several robots have nearby goals. In such cases, planning first the robots that must reach the most crowded goal regions may help avoid situations where robots assigned to peripheral goals introduce reservations that later block access to the denser central area.

The second variant, \textit{Low Radius Density Positions First}, computes the density score using the initial positions of the robots and prioritizes robots that start in less crowded regions. This follows the intuition that, when robots are initially clustered, it may be beneficial to first move the robots located closer to the periphery of the cluster. By freeing these outer positions earlier, the planner may create more space for the robots located in more central and constrained starting regions to find feasible paths afterwards.

Therefore, these two variants use the same radius-density score but apply it to different reference points and with opposite sorting directions. 


\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:radius_neighbor_density_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:radius_neighbor_density} summarizes the \textit{Radius Neighbor Density} heuristic. 

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Radius Neighbor Density Heuristic}
\label{alg:radius_neighbor_density}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Set of robots \(R\), reference function \(\mathrm{ref}(\cdot)\), radius threshold \(R_{\text{th}}\), ordering mode \(mode\)
\Ensure Priority ordering \(\pi\)

\For{each robot \(r_i \in R\)}
    \State \(n_i \gets \mathrm{ref}(r_i)\) \Comment{current position or goal position}
    \State \(score_i \gets 0\)

    \For{each robot \(r_j \in R\) with \(j \neq i\)}
        \State \(n_j \gets \mathrm{ref}(r_j)\)
        \If{\(d(n_i,n_j) \leq R_{\text{th}}\)}
            \State \(score_i \gets score_i + 1\) \Comment{count nearby robot}
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
\EndFor

\State \(\pi \gets \textsc{SortByScore}(R, score, mode)\)

\State \Return \(\pi\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The heuristic computes each density score by comparing the reference point of a robot with the reference points of all remaining robots and counting how many lie within the radius threshold \(R_{\text{th}}\). Since every robot is compared with the remaining \(n-1\) robots, computing all density scores requires \(O(n(n-1)) \approx O(n^2)\) pairwise checks. The final score-based sorting step requires \(O(n \log n)\) time.
Thus, the total computational cost is given by

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:radius_neighbor_density_complexity}
    O(n^2) + O(n \log n).
\end{equation}

It is also important to note that this complexity does not scale with the value of \(R_{\text{th}}\). The radius only affects the outcome of each pairwise comparison, not the number of comparisons performed.

\subsection{\(K\)-hop Neighbor Density}
\label{subsec:K_hop_neighbor_density}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{\(K\)-hop Neighbor Density} heuristic assigns priorities according to the number of robots located within a graph-based neighborhood of each robot. Unlike the \textit{Radius Neighbor Density} heuristic, which defines proximity using a geometric distance threshold, this heuristic defines neighborhoods directly from the roadmap connectivity.

Given a hop limit $k_{\text{hops}}$, let $N_{k_{\text{hops}}}(n_i)$ denote the set of roadmap vertices that can be reached from $n_i$ in at most $k_{\text{hops}}$ graph hops. The score of robot $r_i$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    score_i =
    \sum_{\substack{r_j \in R \\ j \neq i}}
    \kappa(i,j),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\kappa(i,j)=
\begin{cases}
1, & n_j \in N_{k_{\text{hops}}}(n_i), \\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Therefore, the score corresponds to the number of robots whose reference points are reachable from $n_i$ within at most $k_{\text{hops}}$ roadmap edges. Robots with higher scores are located in denser graph-based neighborhoods.
For the configuration illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:khop_neighbor_density}, increasing the hop depth changes the number of neighboring robots counted for $r_1$, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:khop_neighbor_density_example}.

Although conceptually similar to \textit{Radius Neighbor Density}, this heuristic defines neighborhoods through roadmap connectivity rather than Euclidean distance. This distinction is relevant in obstacle-rich environments, where robots may be close in continuous space but far apart on the roadmap due to walls or obstacles that force longer routes.


\subsubsection{Ordering variants}
\label{subsubsec:K_hop_neighbor_density_ordering_variants}
\hspace{\parindent}
The ordering variants follow the same logic as in the \textit{Radius Neighbor Density} heuristic. \textit{Low K-hop Density Positions First} and \textit{High K-hop Density Goals First} differ only in the reference point used and in the sorting direction.
\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:K_hop_neighbor_density_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:khop_density} summarizes the \textit{\(K\)-hop Neighbor Density} heuristic. For each robot, a recursive DFS explores the roadmap from the reference vertex up to \(k_{\text{hops}}\), and the score counts the robot reference vertices within that neighborhood.


\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Recursive DFS \(K\)-hop Expansion}
\label{alg:recursive_khop_expansion}
\footnotesize
\setstretch{0.9}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Roadmap graph \(G=(V,E)\), current vertex \(v\), remaining hops \(h\), map \(bestK\)
\Ensure Updated map \(bestK\)

\If{\(h \leq 0\)}
    \State \Return
\EndIf

\State \(h \gets h - 1\)

\For{each neighbor \(u \in N(v)\)}
    \If{\(u \notin bestK\) or \(h > bestK[u]\)}
        \State \(bestK[u] \gets h\) \Comment{best remaining-hop value found for \(u\)}
        \State \Call{RecursiveKHop}{$G, u, h, bestK$}
    \EndIf
\EndFor

\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{\(K\)-hop Neighbor Density Heuristic}
\label{alg:khop_density}
\footnotesize
\setstretch{0.9}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Set of robots \(R\), roadmap graph \(G=(V,E)\), reference function \(\mathrm{ref}(\cdot)\), hop limit \(k_{\text{hops}}\), ordering mode \(mode\)
\Ensure Priority ordering \(\pi\)

\State \(robotCountAtVertex \gets \emptyset\)

\For{each robot \(r_j \in R\)}
    \State \(n_j \gets \mathrm{ref}(r_j)\) \Comment{current position or goal position}
    \State \(robotCountAtVertex[n_j] \gets robotCountAtVertex[n_j] + 1\) \Comment{count robots at each reference vertex}
\EndFor

\For{each robot \(r_i \in R\)}
    \State \(n_i \gets \mathrm{ref}(r_i)\) \Comment{current position or goal position}
    \State \(bestK \gets \emptyset\)
    \State \(bestK[n_i] \gets k_{\text{hops}}\)
    \State \Call{RecursiveKHop}{$G, n_i, k_{\text{hops}}, bestK$}
    \State \(N_{k_{\text{hops}}}(n_i) \gets\) keys of \(bestK\) \Comment{vertices reachable within \(k_{\text{hops}}\) hops}
    \State \(score_i \gets 0\)

    \For{each vertex \(v \in N_{k_{\text{hops}}}(n_i)\)}
        \State \(score_i \gets score_i + robotCountAtVertex[v]\) \Comment{count robots in the \(k\)-hop neighborhood}
    \EndFor

    \State \(score_i \gets score_i - 1\) \Comment{exclude robot \(r_i\) from its own density score}
\EndFor

\State \(\pi \gets \textsc{SortByScore}(R, score, mode)\)

\State \Return \(\pi\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The computational cost of the \textit{$K$-hop Neighbor Density} heuristic depends on the structure of the roadmap graph, since the number of vertices reached by a $k$-hop expansion is determined by the graph connectivity. In this work, the analysis considers a grid-based roadmap, which is a common map representation in path-planning benchmarks and is also used in the experimental evaluation of this document. In this type of roadmap, each vertex has at most four neighbors.

Assuming an infinite 2D grid with four-neighbor connectivity, the number of vertices at exactly $j$ hops from a reference vertex is $4j$. The number of vertices reachable within $k_{\text{hops}}$ hops is denoted by $N_k$ and, under this grid assumption, is given by
\begin{equation}
    N_k =
    1 + \sum_{j=1}^{k_{\text{hops}}} 4j
    =
    1 + 4 \cdot \frac{k_{\text{hops}}(k_{\text{hops}}+1)}{2}
    =
    1 + 2k_{\text{hops}}(k_{\text{hops}}+1).
    \label{eq:khop_reachable_vertices}
\end{equation}

However, in the recursive implementation, the number of reachable vertices does not necessarily match the number of vertex expansions performed by the algorithm. This occurs because a vertex may be expanded more than once if it is later reached with a larger remaining hop value. The number of vertex expansions performed during one recursive $k$-hop procedure is represented by $X_k$. Since each reachable vertex can be expanded at most $k_{\text{hops}}$ times, a conservative upper bound is
\begin{equation}
    X_k \leq k_{\text{hops}} \cdot N_k.
\end{equation}
This bound is conservative, since in practice many vertices are expanded fewer times, especially in finite grids and near map boundaries. Nevertheless, it provides a safe upper bound for the recursive implementation.
Substituting the expression of $N_k$ gives
\begin{equation}
    X_k \leq
    k_{\text{hops}}
    \left(1 + 2k_{\text{hops}}(k_{\text{hops}}+1)\right).
\end{equation}
Expanding this expression results in
\begin{equation}
    X_k \leq
    2k_{\text{hops}}^3
    + 2k_{\text{hops}}^2
    + k_{\text{hops}}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, the cost of one recursive $k$-hop expansion is upper-bounded by
\begin{equation}
    O(X_k)
    =
    O\left(2k_{\text{hops}}^3 + 2k_{\text{hops}}^2 + k_{\text{hops}}\right),
\end{equation}
which can be simplified to
\begin{equation}
    O(k_{\text{hops}}^3).
\end{equation}
Since one $k$-hop expansion is performed for each robot, the total cost of computing the density scores is
\begin{equation}
    O(n \cdot k_{\text{hops}}^3).
\end{equation}
Finally, the robots are sorted according to their scores, which requires $O(n \log n)$ time. Therefore, the total computational cost of the heuristic is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:k_hop_neighbor_density_complexity}
    O(n \cdot k_{\text{hops}}^3) + O(n \log n).
\end{equation}


As shown by this expression, the computational cost of the \textit{\(K\)-hop Neighbor Density} heuristic depends directly on the selected hop limit \(k_{\text{hops}}\). Therefore, increasing the depth of the graph exploration increases the cost of the heuristic. This contrasts with the \textit{Radius Neighbor Density} heuristic, whose complexity does not depend on the radius threshold \(R_{\text{th}}\), since the radius only affects the result of each pairwise comparison and not the number of comparisons performed.

Consequently, although the \textit{\(K\)-hop Neighbor Density} heuristic may provide a density estimate that is more consistent with the roadmap structure, especially in environments with obstacles, it is expected to become less efficient as \(k_{\text{hops}}\) increases. In such cases, the radius-based heuristic may become computationally more efficient, particularly when larger neighborhoods are considered.

\subsection{Spatial Cluster-Based}
\label{subsec:spatial_cluster_based}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{Spatial Cluster-Based} heuristic groups robots according to the spatial distribution of their reference points. In the base formulation, these reference points correspond to the current positions of the robots, and the resulting heuristic corresponds to the \textit{High Cluster Peripheral First} variant.

DBSCAN is used to obtain the spatial clusters. As discussed in Subsection~\ref{subsubsec:dbscan}, this method can identify dense groups without directly specifying the number of clusters beforehand, while also allowing isolated points to be treated as noise. These properties are useful in the present context, since the number of robot groups may vary across planning instances and isolated robots should not necessarily affect the priority score of dense groups.

The suitability of this choice is supported by the benchmark study of Murugesan et al.~\cite{murugesan2021benchmarking}. Their results show that K-means is more appropriate for compact and convex datasets, whereas DBSCAN is better suited to non-convex data, well-separated dense regions, and datasets where outliers should be explicitly identified. This distinction is relevant for multi-robot planning because robot formations are not always compact around a centroid. Depending on the roadmap and the task configuration, robots may form irregular spatial concentrations along corridors, near bottlenecks, around intersections, or around dense goal regions.

Therefore, DBSCAN is not adopted because it is generally superior to K-means, but because its assumptions better match the intended role of this heuristic. The objective is to detect spatial concentrations of robots that may influence the priority ordering, without requiring a predefined number of groups and without forcing isolated robots into artificial clusters.

After the clusters are computed, the heuristic assigns a priority score to each robot according to the size of its cluster and its relative distance to the cluster centroid. For a robot \(r_i\) belonging to cluster \(C_m\), the centroid of the cluster is denoted by \(c_m\), and the current position of the robot is denoted by \(s_i\). The distance between the robot and the centroid is defined as

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:di}
    d_i = \|s_i - c_m\|_2.
\end{equation}

The maximum distance between the centroid and any robot in the same cluster is defined as

\begin{equation}
    d_{\max,m}
    =
    \max_{r_\ell \in C_m}
    \|s_\ell - c_m\|_2.
\end{equation}

The priority score of robot $r_i$ is then computed as

\begin{equation}
    score_i
    =
    |C_m|
    \cdot
    \frac{d_i}{d_{\max,m}},
    \qquad d_{\max,m} \neq 0
    \label{eq:cluster_periph_score}
\end{equation}
where $|C_m|$ is the number of robots in cluster $C_m$.

The score contains two components. The factor $|C_m|$ increases the priority of robots belonging to larger clusters, reflecting the assumption that larger groups are typically more difficult to resolve in a prioritized planner. Within each cluster, the normalized distance term increases the score of robots farther from the centroid. As a result, the heuristic first favors robots in larger clusters and, inside each cluster, prioritizes those located closer to the boundary.

Figure~\ref{fig:spatial_cluster_based} illustrates the scoring principle. Cluster $C_1$ contains more robots than cluster $C_2$, and therefore receives a higher cluster-size contribution. Within each cluster, the distance to the centroid determines the relative ordering of the robots according to the selected variant.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{clustering.png}
    \caption{High Cluster Peripheral First scoring example.}
    \label{fig:spatial_cluster_based}
\end{figure}


\subsubsection{Ordering variants}
\label{subsubsec:spatial_cluster_based_ordering_variants}
\hspace{\parindent}
Two ordering variants were considered for the Spatial Cluster-Based heuristic. The first one is \textit{High Cluster Peripheral First}, which corresponds to the formulation described above. This variant clusters robots according to their current positions and favors robots located farther from the centroid of larger clusters.

The second variant is \textit{High Cluster Central Goal First}. This variant applies the same clustering principle to the goal positions, but reverses the intra-cluster preference by favoring robots whose goals are closer to the centroid of their goal cluster.

For this variant, \(g_i\) represents the goal position of robot \(r_i\), and \(d_i = \|g_i - c_m\|_2\) is its distance to the centroid of the corresponding goal cluster. The priority score is defined as

\begin{equation}
    score_i
    =
    \frac{|C_m|}{d_i},
    \qquad d_i \neq 0
    \label{eq:cluster_central_score}
\end{equation}
where \(|C_m|\) is the size of the goal cluster.
When \(d_i = 0\), the robot is assigned the highest priority within the cluster, since its goal
coincides with the cluster centroid.
Unlike the peripheral variant, where the score increases with the distance to the centroid, the central-goal variant assigns higher scores to robots closer to the centroid. Thus, both variants use cluster size to favor larger groups, but differ in the selected reference point and in the intra-cluster ordering criterion.

\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:spatial_cluster_based_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:spatial_cluster_based} summarizes the \textit{Spatial Cluster-Based} heuristic. The algorithm extracts the selected reference point of each robot and applies DBSCAN to identify spatial groups of nearby robots. Since DBSCAN may classify isolated points as noise, robots outside any cluster keep their initial score, set to zero in the implementation. Thus, only clustered robots are affected by the clustering component, with scores computed from the cluster size and the distance to the corresponding centroid.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Spatial Cluster-Based Heuristic}
\label{alg:spatial_cluster_based}
\footnotesize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Set of robots $R$, reference function $\mathrm{ref}(\cdot)$, score function $\mathrm{score}(\cdot)$, ordering mode $mode$
\Ensure Priority ordering $\pi$

\State $P \gets \emptyset$ \Comment{reference points used for clustering}

\For{each robot $r_i \in R$}
    \State $q_i \gets \mathrm{ref}(r_i)$ \Comment{current position or goal position}
    \State add $(q_i, \mathrm{id}(r_i))$ to $P$
    \State $score_i \gets 0$
\EndFor

\State $C \gets \textsc{DBSCAN}(P)$ \Comment{spatial clusters of robot reference points}

\For{each cluster $C_m \in C$}
    \State $c_m \gets$ centroid of $C_m$
    \State $d_{\max,m} \gets 0$

    \For{each point $q_i \in C_m$}
        \State $d_i \gets \|q_i - c_m\|_2$
        \State $d_{\max,m} \gets \max(d_{\max,m}, d_i)$
    \EndFor

    \For{each point $q_i \in C_m$ associated with robot $r_i$}
        \State $score_i \gets \mathrm{score}(|C_m|, d_i, d_{\max,m})$
    \EndFor
\EndFor

\State $\pi \gets \textsc{SortByScore}(R, score, mode)$

\State \Return $\pi$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}



The algorithm starts by building the set of reference points, which requires processing each robot once and therefore has \(O(n)\) time complexity.

The computational cost of DBSCAN depends on the implementation used to perform neighborhood queries. In the implementation considered in this work, the \(\varepsilon\)-neighborhood of each point is obtained by scanning all other points. Since there are at most \(n\) points, each neighborhood query costs \(O(n)\), and the complete clustering step has complexity \(O(n^2)\).

After clustering, the heuristic computes the centroid of each cluster, the distance between each cluster member and the centroid, and the maximum distance inside the cluster. Since each robot belongs to at most one cluster, the sum of the cluster sizes is at most \(n\). Therefore, the total score-computation cost over all clusters is \(O(n)\). Thus, the total computational cost is given by

\begin{equation}
   O(n^2) + O(n) + O(n \log n).
\end{equation}




\section{Conflict-Based Heuristics}
\label{sec:conflicts_heuristics}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{Conflict-Based} heuristics developed in this work are motivated by the conflict and
interaction criteria discussed in Section~\ref{sec:agent_prioritization}. As described in that
section, previous work has considered interaction mostly through indirect indicators, such as the
effect of existing plans on route cost~\cite{termors2011evaluating}. In contrast, the heuristics
proposed here use predicted space-time conflicts explicitly as priority information.

This information is obtained by first computing an individual path for each robot while ignoring the
remaining robots. The resulting paths are then compared over time to identify predicted interactions
between pairs of robots.

Figure~\ref{fig:conflict_overview} illustrates this process by combining the roadmap scenario with
one possible set of independently computed single-robot \(A^*\) paths. Figure~\ref{fig:conflict_scenario}
shows the initial robot and goal configuration, while Figure~\ref{fig:single_robot_conflicts}
shows the predicted space-time conflicts obtained from comparing the individual paths over time.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{subfigure}[t]{0.41\linewidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{conflict_scenario.png}
        \caption{Conflict scenario.}
        \label{fig:conflict_scenario}
    \end{subfigure}
    \hfill
    \begin{subfigure}[t]{0.49\linewidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{single_robot_conflicts.png}
        \caption{Single-robot $A^*$ paths and predicted conflicts.}
        \label{fig:single_robot_conflicts}
    \end{subfigure}
    \caption{Illustrative example for the conflict-based heuristics.}
    \label{fig:conflict_overview}
\end{figure}

For each robot \(r_i\), the associated conflict occurrences are represented by the set \(O_i\). Each conflict occurrence contributes to the priority score through a coefficient \(\alpha\). The priority score is then defined as

\begin{equation}
    score_i
    =
    \sum_{c \in O_i} \alpha^{t}_{R_c},
    \label{eq:conflict_based_general_score}
\end{equation}
where \(R_c\) denotes the set of robots involved in conflict occurrence \(c\), and \(t\) denotes the time step at which the conflict occurs.

Different conflict-based heuristics can be obtained by changing the definition of \(\alpha\). The underlying intuition is that robots involved in more relevant predicted conflicts are more likely to be difficult to coordinate in a prioritized planner such as TEA*. By assigning priorities according to conflict information, these heuristics attempt to plan first or last the robots whose individual paths are expected to interfere more strongly with the rest of the system.

\subsection{Space-Time Conflicts}
\label{subsec:space_time_conflicts}
\hspace{\parindent}
The Space-Time Conflicts heuristic corresponds to the simplest conflict-based variant. In this case, all predicted conflict occurrences are treated with the same importance. Therefore, each conflict occurrence has unit weight
\begin{equation}
    \alpha^{t}_{R_c} = 1.
\end{equation}
Substituting this definition into Equation~\ref{eq:conflict_based_general_score}, the priority score of robot $r_i$ becomes
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{score}(r_i)
    =
    |O_i|.
\end{equation}

Thus, the score is simply the number of predicted space-time conflicts involving robot $r_i$. Robots involved in more conflicts receive higher priority scores, since their individual paths are expected to interfere more frequently with the paths of the remaining robots.
For the example shown in Figure~\ref{fig:single_robot_conflicts}, the predicted conflict occurrences are
\[
    c_{2,3}^{t=1}, \qquad
    c_{3,4}^{t=2}, \qquad
    c_{1,2}^{t=3}.
\]
Since each conflict has unit weight, the resulting scores are
\[
    score_1 = 1, \qquad
    score_2 = 2, \qquad
    score_3 = 2, \qquad
    score_4 = 1.
\]
Therefore, robots \(r_2\) and \(r_3\) obtain the highest conflict scores in this example, while robots \(r_1\) and \(r_4\) obtain lower scores. However, this score should be interpreted only as a static estimate of conflict involvement. The heuristic does not account for the fact that resolving an earlier conflict may change the subsequent paths and consequently remove conflicts that were originally predicted.

\subsubsection{Ordering variants}
\label{subsubsec:space_time_conflicts_ordering_variants}
\hspace{\parindent}
Two ordering variants were considered for the \textit{Space-Time Conflicts} heuristic. In the \textit{More Conflicts First} variant, robots are sorted by decreasing score, so robots involved in more predicted conflicts are planned earlier. For the example in Figure~\ref{fig:single_robot_conflicts}, this produces the priority grouping \(\pi = \langle \{r_2, r_3\}, \{r_1, r_4\} \rangle\).

In the \textit{Less Conflicts First} variant, the ordering is reversed, and robots with fewer predicted conflicts are planned earlier. The resulting priority grouping is \(\pi = \langle \{r_1, r_4\}, \{r_2, r_3\} \rangle\).

\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:space_time_conflicts_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
The conflict extraction step is common to the conflict-based heuristics. Algorithm~\ref{alg:predicted_conflict_extraction}
computes an independent single-robot path for each robot and compares the resulting paths over time
to obtain the set of predicted conflict occurrences \(O_i\) associated with each robot.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Predicted Conflict Extraction}
\label{alg:predicted_conflict_extraction}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Set of robots \(R\), current positions \(s_i\), goal positions \(g_i\)
\Ensure Conflict occurrence sets \(O_i\) for all robots \(r_i \in R\)

\For{each robot \(r_i \in R\)}
    \State \(p_i \gets A^*(s_i, g_i)\) \Comment{single-robot shortest path on the roadmap}
    \State \(O_i \gets \emptyset\) \Comment{conflict occurrences involving robot \(r_i\)}
\EndFor

\State \(L_{\max} \gets \max_i |p_i|\)

\For{\(t = 0, 1, \ldots, L_{\max}-1\)}
    \For{each pair of robots \((r_i,r_j)\) with \(i < j\)}
        \If{\(t < |p_i|\) \textbf{and} \(t < |p_j|\)}
            \State \(e_i(t) \gets\) edge traversed by robot \(r_i\) at timestep \(t\)
            \State \(e_j(t) \gets\) edge traversed by robot \(r_j\) at timestep \(t\)
            \State \(V_{\mathrm{conf}} \gets\) shared endpoint vertices of \(e_i(t)\) and \(e_j(t)\)

            \If{\(V_{\mathrm{conf}} \neq \emptyset\)}
                \State add conflict occurrence \((t,V_{\mathrm{conf}},r_j)\) to \(O_i\)
                \State add conflict occurrence \((t,V_{\mathrm{conf}},r_i)\) to \(O_j\)
            \EndIf
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
\EndFor

\State \Return \(\{O_i\}_{r_i \in R}\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


For each robot, the heuristic first computes a single-robot shortest path on the roadmap using \(A^*\), and the resulting independent paths are then used to estimate the number of space-time conflicts involving each robot.

Using the graph-search complexity given in Equation~\ref{eq:dijkstra_complexity}, the path generation step is applied once per robot.

After the individual paths are obtained, the heuristic compares them over time. The maximum path length among all robots is denoted by \(L_{\max} = \max_i |p_i|\). For each timestep, all unordered pairs of robots are checked for conflicts. Since there are \(O(n^2)\) robot pairs and each pairwise conflict check requires constant time, the conflict detection step costs

\begin{equation}
    O(L_{\max} \cdot n^2).
\end{equation}

The score of each robot is obtained by counting the conflict occurrences stored in \(O_i\). This operation is bounded by the number of detected conflicts and, in the worst case, does not exceed the pairwise conflict detection cost. The final score-based sorting step requires \(O(n \log n)\) time. Thus, the total computational cost is given by

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:space_time_conflicts_heuristic}
    O\bigl(n \cdot (|V| + |E|)\log |V|\bigr)
    + O\bigl(L_{\max} \cdot n^2\bigr)
    + O(n \log n).
\end{equation}



\subsection{Time-DoF Weighted Conflicts}
\label{subsec:time_dof_weighted_conflicts}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{Time-DoF Weighted Conflicts} heuristic extends the previous conflict-based formulation by assigning a different weight to each predicted conflict occurrence. The objective is to distinguish conflicts not only by how often they occur, but also by when and where they occur along the individually planned paths.

For each conflict occurrence \(c \in O_i\), the timestep at which the conflict occurs is denoted by \(t_c\), the set of robots involved is denoted by \(R_c\), and the set of vertices associated with the conflict is denoted by \(V_c\). The coefficient assigned to occurrence \(c\) is defined as

\begin{equation}
    \alpha^{t_c}_{R_c}
    =
    \frac{1}{\min\limits_{v \in V_c}\mathrm{DoF}(v)}
    \cdot
    \frac{1}{1+t_c},
    \label{eq:time_dof_conflict_weight}
\end{equation}
where \(V_c\) contains the vertices associated with the conflict occurrence. The term \(\min_{v \in V_c}\mathrm{DoF}(v)\) makes the coefficient depend on the most constrained vertex involved in the conflict. For vertex conflicts, this corresponds to the shared vertex. For edge interactions, \(V_c\) includes the vertices associated with the interacting edges, and the minimum DoF is used to represent the most constrained part of that interaction.

The temporal term gives higher weight to conflicts that occur earlier in the individual paths, based on the assumption that the first steps are more likely to be executed as originally computed. As time progresses, reservations introduced by other robots may force path changes, making later predicted conflicts less reliable indicators of actual future interference. This partially addresses a limitation of the previous heuristic, where all conflicts were counted equally, even though resolving an earlier conflict may alter subsequent paths and remove conflicts that were initially detected.

The DoF term gives higher weight to conflicts occurring in more constrained roadmap regions. When a conflict involves vertices with low DoF, the affected robots have fewer alternative movements available around that location. Such conflicts are therefore considered more critical than conflicts occurring in highly connected regions, where robots may have more possible escape routes or alternative paths.

Substituting the weight of Equation~\ref{eq:time_dof_conflict_weight} into the general conflict-based score defined in Equation~\ref{eq:conflict_based_general_score}, the score of robot $r_i$ becomes
\begin{equation}
    score_i
    =
    \sum_{c \in O_i}
    \left(
    \frac{1}{\min\limits_{v \in V_c}\mathrm{DoF}(v)}
    \cdot
    \frac{1}{1+t_c}
    \right).
    \label{eq:time_dof_weighted_conflict_score}
\end{equation}

For the example shown in Figure~\ref{fig:single_robot_conflicts}, assume that all vertices involved in the predicted conflicts have \(\mathrm{DoF}(v)=4\). Under this assumption, the coefficients are determined only by the timestep of each conflict:
\[
    \alpha_{2,3}^{t=1}
    =
    \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{1}{1+1}
    =
    \frac{1}{8},
    \qquad
    \alpha_{3,4}^{t=2}
    =
    \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{1}{1+2}
    =
    \frac{1}{12},
    \qquad
    \alpha_{1,2}^{t=3}
    =
    \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{1}{1+3}
    =
    \frac{1}{16}.
\]
The resulting weighted scores are
\[
    score_1 = \frac{1}{16}, \qquad
    score_2 = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{16} = \frac{3}{16}, \qquad
    score_3 = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{12} = \frac{5}{24}, \qquad
    score_4 = \frac{1}{12}.
\]


\subsubsection{Ordering variants}
\label{subsubsec:time_dof_weighted_conflicts_ordering_variants}
\hspace{\parindent}
Two ordering variants were considered. \textit{More Time-DoF Weighted Conflicts First} plans robots
with higher weighted conflict scores earlier, giving priority to conflicts that are predicted to
occur earlier and in more constrained roadmap regions. For the example shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:single_robot_conflicts}, this produces the priority ordering
\(\pi = \langle r_3, r_2, r_4, r_1 \rangle\).

Figure~\ref{fig:weighted_conflicts_teastar} shows a possible conflict-free set of paths generated by
TEA* using this ordering, first as vertex sequences over time and then as a projection onto the
roadmap structure. The sequence view makes the waiting actions explicit, while the projected view
shows how the same paths evolve through space and time.

In this example, the priority ordering allows TEA* to introduce waiting actions that avoid the
predicted conflicts shown in the independently computed paths. Robot \(r_2\) waits at timestep
\(t=1\), while robot \(r_4\) waits at timestep \(t=2\). These waits change the temporal alignment of
the paths and resolve the earlier interactions involving robots \(r_2\), \(r_3\), and \(r_4\).

As a consequence, the later predicted conflict between \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) at timestep \(t=3\),
visible in Figure~\ref{fig:single_robot_conflicts}, no longer occurs in the final TEA* plan, since
the waiting action introduced for \(r_2\) changes its subsequent position sequence. This illustrates
why later predicted conflicts should be weighted less than earlier ones, as they may disappear once
previous interactions have been resolved.

This behavior motivates the temporal component of the \textit{Time-DoF Weighted Conflicts}
heuristic. By assigning lower weight to later conflicts, the heuristic gives more influence to
interactions that are more likely to remain relevant during the final prioritized planning process.
In the \textit{Less Time-DoF Weighted Conflicts First} variant, the ordering is reversed, with lower
weighted conflict scores planned earlier.

\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:time_dof_weighted_conflicts_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{Time-DoF Weighted Conflicts} heuristic uses the same predicted conflict sets \(O_i\)
computed by Algorithm~\ref{alg:predicted_conflict_extraction}, but replaces the unit conflict weight
with a coefficient that depends on the conflict timestep and on the DoF of the associated roadmap
vertices. The value \(\mathrm{DoF}(v)\) is computed as defined in Section~\ref{sec:topological_heuristics},
by counting the distinct roadmap neighbors of vertex \(v\).

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Time-DoF Weighted Conflicts Scoring}
\label{alg:time_dof_weighted_conflicts}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Set of robots \(R\), conflict occurrence sets \(O_i\), ordering mode \(mode\)
\Ensure Priority ordering \(\pi\)

\For{each robot \(r_i \in R\)}
    \State \(score_i \gets 0\)

    \For{each occurrence \((t,V_{\mathrm{conf}},r_j) \in O_i\)}
        \State \(DoF_{\min} \gets \displaystyle\min_{v \in V_{\mathrm{conf}}} \mathrm{DoF}(v)\)
        \State \(\alpha \gets \dfrac{1}{DoF_{\min}} \cdot \dfrac{1}{1+t}\)
        \State \(score_i \gets score_i + \alpha\)
    \EndFor
\EndFor

\State \(\pi \gets \textsc{SortByScore}(R, score, mode)\)
\State \Return \(\pi\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The \textit{Time-DoF Weighted Conflicts} heuristic follows the same general structure as the
\textit{Space-Time Conflicts} heuristic, but each detected conflict is additionally weighted using
time and DoF information. Since the DoF computation adds a factor bounded by the maximum roadmap
degree \(\Delta\), the total computational cost becomes

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:time_dof_weighted_conflicts_heuristic}
    O\bigl(n \cdot (|V| + |E|)\log |V|\bigr)
    + O\bigl(L_{\max} \cdot n^2 \cdot (1+\Delta)\bigr)
    + O(n \log n).
\end{equation}


\section{Replanning Heuristics}
\label{sec:replanning_heuristics}
\hspace{\parindent}
The replanning heuristic developed in this work is motivated by the rescheduling strategies discussed
in Section~\ref{sec:agent_prioritization}, where feedback from previous planning attempts is used to
adjust the priority ordering.

In this work, this idea is adapted to the behavior of TEA*. Rather than relying only on whether a
robot failed to find a path, the proposed heuristic uses search statistics collected during a
previous planning attempt. In particular, it considers how strongly each robot was affected by
reservation conflicts during the TEA* search, using this information to compute a revised ordering
for a subsequent planning attempt.

The resulting heuristic, named \textit{Exploration Conflicts Pressure}, is therefore tailored to the
information naturally produced by a prioritized planner. It uses replanning feedback to identify
robots whose search was more constrained by previous reservations, allowing the next priority
ordering to reflect the difficulties observed in the previous attempt.

\subsection{Exploration Conflicts Pressure}
\label{subsec:exploration_conflicts_pressure}
\hspace{\parindent}
The \textit{Exploration Conflicts Pressure} heuristic is designed for replanning scenarios, especially when a previous TEA* attempt fails. Unlike the initial-ordering heuristics, this heuristic does not estimate difficulty from distances, topology, local density, or predicted conflicts between independently planned paths. Instead, it uses runtime feedback collected during the actual prioritized planning process to revise the robot ordering.

The main idea is to identify robots whose search was strongly constrained by reservations introduced by previously planned robots. During TEA*, a candidate expansion may be rejected if the corresponding vertex or edge is already reserved in space-time by a higher-priority robot. If this happens frequently during the search of robot $r_i$, it suggests that the robot was negatively affected by its position in the previous ordering. Rather than simply reversing the previous permutation, the heuristic performs a targeted reordering, moving earlier only the robots that show evidence of being constrained by existing reservations.

For robot \(r_i\), \(N_i^{\mathrm{rej}}\) denotes the number of reservation-based rejections observed during the search, and \(E_i\) denotes the total number of expanded nodes. Instead of using \(N_i^{\mathrm{rej}}\) directly, the heuristic normalizes this value by the total exploration effort \(E_i\). This avoids assigning high scores only because a robot required a longer or more extensive search, which naturally creates more opportunities for reservation conflicts to occur. The priority score assigned to robot \(r_i\) is therefore defined as

\begin{equation}
    score_i
    =
    \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{N_i^{\mathrm{rej}}}{E_i}, & \text{if } E_i > 0, \\
    0, & \text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:exploration_conflicts_pressure_score}
\end{equation}

This score measures the fraction of the search effort affected by reservation conflicts. Higher values indicate that a larger proportion of the attempted exploration was blocked by existing space-time reservations, rather than simply indicating that the robot performed a larger search. Therefore, robots with higher scores are interpreted as being more constrained by the previous ordering and may benefit from receiving higher priority in a subsequent planning attempt.

Figure~\ref{fig:exploration_conflicts_pressure} illustrates the type of runtime information used by this heuristic. In the represented TEA* expansion step, robot $r_2$ is being planned after other robots have already introduced space-time reservations. Three possible successor expansions are considered. Two of them are rejected because the corresponding roadmap resources are already reserved by previously planned robots, while only one expansion remains feasible. Therefore, this step contributes two reservation-based rejections to the value of $N_2^{\mathrm{rej}}$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{exploration_conflicts_pressure.png}
    \caption{TEA* expansion step illustrating two reservation-based rejections.}
    \label{fig:exploration_conflicts_pressure}
\end{figure}

This example shows why the ratio in Equation~\ref{eq:exploration_conflicts_pressure_score} can be useful during replanning. If similar rejections occur frequently throughout the search of a robot, this indicates that the robot was strongly affected by reservations imposed by higher-priority robots. The heuristic then uses this information to adjust the ordering in a subsequent planning attempt.

\subsubsection{Core Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:exploration_conflicts_pressure_pseudocode}
\hspace{\parindent}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:exploration_conflicts_pressure} summarizes the \textit{Exploration Conflicts
Pressure} heuristic, which is applied only in a retry context. The heuristic uses statistics from a
previous TEA* attempt, namely the number of expanded space-time nodes \(E_i\) and the number of
reservation-based rejections \(N_i^{\mathrm{rej}}\) observed while planning each robot \(r_i\).

No additional path computation or pairwise conflict enumeration is performed, since the heuristic
relies only on statistics already collected during the previous TEA* execution.
\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Exploration Conflicts Pressure Heuristic}
\label{alg:exploration_conflicts_pressure}
\footnotesize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Planning queue \(Q\), search statistics \(S\) from the previous planning attempt, ordering mode \(mode\)
\Ensure Revised priority ordering \(\pi\)

\For{each robot \(r_i \in Q\)}
    \If{\(r_i \notin S\) \textbf{or} \(S[r_i].E_i = 0\)}
        \State \(score_i \gets 0\)
    \Else
        \State \(N_i^{\mathrm{rej}} \gets S[r_i].N_i^{\mathrm{rej}}\) \Comment{reservation-based rejections}
        \State \(E_i \gets S[r_i].E_i\) \Comment{expanded nodes}
        \State \(score_i \gets \dfrac{N_i^{\mathrm{rej}}}{E_i}\)
    \EndIf
\EndFor

\State \(\pi \gets \textsc{SortByScore}(Q, score, mode)\)

\State \Return \(\pi\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The number of robots in the planning queue is denoted by \(n = |Q|\). For each robot, the heuristic retrieves the corresponding search statistics and computes the ratio between reservation-based rejections and expanded nodes. Assuming that the statistics are accessed in constant time, the score computation step requires \(O(n)\) time.

The final score-based sorting step requires \(O(n \log n)\) time. Thus, the total computational cost is given by

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exploration_conflicts_pressure_complexity}
    O(n) + O(n \log n).
\end{equation}

The cost of this heuristic is small compared with the TEA* planning attempt that produced the statistics. Its practical overhead is therefore limited, since it only reorders the robots using information already collected during the previous search.


\section{Summary}
\label{sec:heuristics_summary}
\hspace{\parindent}
This chapter presented the prioritization heuristics developed to generate alternative robot
orderings for the TEA* planner. Since TEA* plans robots sequentially, the priority order directly
affects the reservations introduced in the space-time search space and, consequently, the feasibility
and quality of the resulting solution.

The proposed heuristics were organized into five groups, each capturing a different source of
planning difficulty. Roadmap distance heuristics use travel-distance information, topological
heuristics evaluate movement flexibility along estimated paths, surroundings heuristics consider
the spatial distribution of robots, conflict-based heuristics use predicted space-time interactions,
and replanning heuristics exploit feedback collected from previous TEA* attempts. These groups are
not intended to define a single dominant priority rule, but rather to provide complementary criteria
for constructing diverse robot orderings.

Table~\ref{tab:heuristic_conceptual_summary} summarizes the conceptual role of each heuristic,
highlighting the priority criterion used by each family. Table~\ref{tab:heuristic_score_complexity_summary}
then provides the corresponding score functions and computational complexities. In both cases, the
reported score defines the value assigned to each robot, while the final priority ordering also
depends on the selected sorting mode, as defined in
Section~\ref{sec:heuristic_design_methodology}.

The computational analysis shows that the heuristics differ substantially in the amount of
pre-planning effort they require. Direct distance and feedback-based criteria introduce limited
overhead, while heuristics that depend on graph searches, neighborhood expansion, clustering, or
predicted conflict extraction are more expensive. This distinction is important because heuristic
computation is performed before TEA* execution, meaning that a more informative ordering is only
useful if its computational cost remains justified by the improvement obtained during planning.

Overall, the chapter defines a diverse set of priority-ordering mechanisms and establishes their
formal scoring rules and computational implications. The following chapter evaluates these
heuristics experimentally, focusing on how their different sources of information affect planning
feasibility, solution quality, and runtime across distinct coordination scenarios.
